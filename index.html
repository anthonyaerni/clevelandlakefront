Cleveland Browns to Drop Name, Rebrand as the Cleveland Steamers in $180M Identity Overhaul

Team ownership cites "generational opportunity" to modernize; critics say history is being steamrolled along with Paul Brown's legacy.
By Donna Kowalczyk, Sports Correspondent  |  Donna covers the Browns and high school football from her home in Parma Heights. Reach her at dkowalczyk@lakegazette.com or (216) 555-0188.
Published 7:14 a.m. · Updated 9:02 a.m. · March 31, 2026
FirstEnergy Stadium, Cleveland, OH  |  Photo: Dave Mosinski / Gazette
The Haslam family held a press conference Monday on the stadium floor where a new "Cleveland Steamers" logo was unveiled on the center-field jumbotron.

CLEVELAND — In a press conference Monday morning that drew a packed crowd of reporters, season-ticket holders, and at least a dozen confused-looking men in old Dawg Pound gear, the Cleveland Browns announced they will officially change the franchise's name to the Cleveland Steamers beginning with the 2026 NFL season — marking the most dramatic rebrand in the franchise's 79-year history.

Team owner Jimmy Haslam, flanked by NFL Commissioner Roger Goodell and a beaming set of marketing consultants from a New York firm whose name nobody could pronounce, said the decision was years in the making and was driven by a desire to "move the organization forward into a new era of Cleveland pride."

"Cleveland is a city of industry. Of grit. Of steam and steel and the Cuyahoga River — and yes, we know what that river did in 1969," Haslam said to nervous laughter from the press pool. "But that fire, that energy — that's what this city is. The Steamers represents who we are."

"Paul Brown's name is on that wall forever. This is about what the next 80 years look like, not the last 80." — Jimmy Haslam, Browns / Steamers Owner

The announcement, first reported by WEWS NewsChannel 5 late Sunday night, sent shockwaves through Northeast Ohio and ignited a firestorm on local talk radio. 92.3 The Fan reported its highest listener volume since the 2016 World Series by 6 a.m., with callers overwhelmingly negative. One man from Strongsville, who identified himself only as "Buckeye Mike," told hosts he was "done. Done. I'm a Lions fan now."

📋 Cleveland Browns / Steamers — Fast Facts

  • Founded: 1946 — one of the original AAFC franchises
  • Named after: Coach Paul Brown, who built the team from scratch
  • Home stadium: FirstEnergy Stadium (aka "The Factory of Sadness"), capacity 67,431
  • Championships: 8 total (4 AAFC, 4 NFL — none since 1964)
  • All-time greats: Jim Brown, Otto Graham, Ozzie Newsome, Bernie Kosar
  • Mascot: Swagger the Dog (debuted 2015; fate under new name unclear)
  • Current head coach: Kevin Stefanski (entering Year 7)
  • 2025 record: 8–9, missed playoffs in Wild Card tiebreaker

The team says the rebrand, valued at approximately $180 million when accounting for stadium signage, merchandise licensing, digital infrastructure, and what Haslam called "community activation," will roll out in phases. New uniforms — described in the press materials as featuring "deep locomotive navy, heritage rust, and whistle silver" — will be unveiled at a fan event at Tower City Amphitheater in June.

The helmets, longtime the most iconic element of the Browns' identity — a plain orange with no logo, unique in the entire NFL — will reportedly be replaced with a stylized steam engine emblem. That detail, perhaps more than any other, drew immediate fury from the fan base.

"You don't touch the helmets," said Gina Partipilo, 54, of Lakewood, who was among the protestors who had already gathered outside the stadium by 9 a.m., waving signs that read "KEEP THE ORANGE" and "HASLAM GO STEAM YOURSELF." "My dad wore that orange helmet to every game from 1974 to 1997. That's not branding. That's religion."

The Haslam family, who purchased the team in 2012 for $1 billion, has faced persistent criticism over their stewardship of the franchise, which has cycled through 16 starting quarterbacks since their purchase. Yet Monday's press conference was notably polished — a far cry from earlier fumbles — with slick video packages, a custom logo reveal, and a pre-recorded message from Mayor Justin Bibb calling Cleveland "a city that doesn't fear reinvention."

City Council member Jenny Spencer, who represents West Side wards including Kamm's Corners and West Park, was not as warm. "Nobody asked the fans," Spencer said in a statement released Monday afternoon. "This is a football team, not a startup. You don't just rebrand 79 years of history because some consultant told you the name tested well in focus groups."

"Nobody asked the fans. You don't rebrand 79 years of history because some consultant told you the name tested well in focus groups." — Councilwoman Jenny Spencer, Cleveland West Side

The NFL confirmed it approved the name change earlier this month in a closed session. The league has been open to franchise rebrands in recent years, most notably the Washington Commanders and Las Vegas Raiders moves, though the Browns situation is unique given the franchise's history of returning from the dead — the original Browns were relocated to Baltimore in 1995, becoming the Ravens, before a new expansion Browns franchise was granted to Cleveland in 1999.

Paul Brown's family has not issued a public statement as of press time. A spokesperson for the Paul Brown estate said only that the family was "reviewing the matter with counsel."

Quarterback DeShaun Watson, who remains under contract despite ongoing injury concerns, appeared briefly at the press conference in a Steamers hat and said he was "excited for a fresh start." He then declined to take questions.

The team's new name is not without its, shall we say, unintended associations. Within minutes of the announcement, social media lit up with jokes — many not printable in a family newspaper — about the phrase "Cleveland Steamer," which carries a well-documented secondary meaning in American slang. Haslam appeared either unaware of or unconcerned by the double entendre, though a clearly uncomfortable PR handler could be seen mouthing something into her headset when the first reporter raised the issue.

When pressed directly by a reporter from Cleveland.com, Haslam said, "We've heard that, and frankly, it's childish. Cleveland is a world-class city. We're not going to let juvenile internet humor define us." He then pivoted to discussing merchandise pre-order availability, which he said would go live on the team's new website — steamers.com — by Friday.

Browns legend and Pro Football Hall of Famer Jim Brown, who passed away in 2023, once said that Cleveland football was "not about a name on the front of the jersey, but the city stitched into the soul of every player who wore it." It is a quote that longtime fans are already circulating widely today — and one that feels, in this particular moment, more loaded than ever.

Season ticket holders received an email Monday morning outlining a "Loyalty Transition Package," which includes a 15% discount on new Steamers merchandise and the option to keep their existing seat assignments through 2028. Multiple season ticket holders told the Gazette they had already contacted the team to inquire about cancellations.

The Lakefront Gazette will provide continued coverage of this story as it develops. A community forum is scheduled for Thursday evening at Parma High School's auditorium, hosted by Councilwoman Spencer, open to all residents.

🏈 Browns History

Founded: 1946 by Arthur "Mickey" McBride. Named after first head coach Paul Brown.

Last NFL Championship: 1964 — a 27–0 shutout of the Baltimore Colts at Cleveland Municipal Stadium.

#32 Jim Brown remains widely considered the greatest running back in NFL history.

The Relocation: In 1995, Art Modell moved the team to Baltimore. City of Cleveland fought in court to retain the name, colors, and history. The "new" Browns returned in 1999.

The Helmet: The plain orange lid, introduced in 1946, is the only helmet in the NFL with no logo — a point of pride for 79 years.
